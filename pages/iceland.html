Iceland

Day 1: Reykjavik & Blue Lagoon

Taking a Redeye from D.C. to Iceland, I landed around 4:30 AM local time. First thing's first was coffee, a quick bite at 10-11 (Iceland's 7/11) and then catching the 7:30 AM transfer bus from KEF to Blue Lagoon via Destination Blue Lagoon. Walking out and seeing Iceland for the first time was a surreal feeling - the weather was foggy but the sun was still peeking out (felt like I was in a sci-fi movie)!

I got to Blue Lagoon at 8 AM, took a dip, went to the sauna and grabbed food before heading to my hostel at HI Eco Hostel. I felt tired from traveling, so after some quick exploring and dinner, I headed back to rest.

Micro-lessons

  • Bring a charging adapter: I did not bring one so I bought one from the Corner Market near my hostel.

  • Booking transfer from Blue Lagoon: Reykjavik has predefined bus stops that you can choose to get dropped off at. All you need to do is find the stop closest to your hotel/hostel!

  • Go straight from Airport to the Blue Lagoon: Most flights from the U.S. to Reykjavik are red eyes and going to any lagoon (e.g., sky lagoon, secret lagoon) helps you fight the jetlag. And kill time if your check-in isn't until the afternoon.

Day 2: Hvammsvik Hot Springs

The next day, I visited Hvammsvik, a Hot Spring Resort. Hvammsvik has six hot springs that are slightly spread out.

I was treated with so much kindness and hospitality by the staff. I forgot my sneakers in the changing room and when I called the front desk, the manager was willing to drive the shoes into Reykjavik and drop them off right outside of my hostel.

Micro-lessons

  • Bring or rent water shoes: If you visit Hvammsvik, water shoes come in handy when you walk between hot springs.

  • Double check your transfers: I missed my original transfer to Hvammsvik because I mixed up the pick up time. The manager was nice enough to put me on the evening transfer.

My next destination was visiting Iceland's South Coast, which is a popular day trip out from Reykjavik. I booked a small group tour through Viator, which took us out to the top stops (8-12 people total traveling via sprinter van). If you are traveling solo, this can be a good way to meet new people since there's usually at least one or two other solo travelers in these groups. Some top spots I visited were:

  1. Reynisfjara black sand beach with its basalt columns and powerful waves
  2. Seljalandsfoss waterfall where you can walk behind the cascading water
  3. Skógafoss waterfall, one of Iceland's largest and most impressive
  4. Sólheimajökull glacier, a stunning outlet glacier of Mýrdalsjökull

Day 4: Snæfellsnes Peninsula

The last outing was to Snæfellsnes Peninsula. I booked a tour through Troll.is, which overall was my favorite. My tour was led by a spunky pink-haired lady named Cora. She shared interesting tidbits like how the bumpy road we were on was nicknamed the "kidney stone removers", and how recent a volcanic eruption is easily identified by how much moss has grown atop the hardened lava. Most memorable was her sharing her journey from her home in Romania to Iceland, her past 10+ years building a life here, and watching her eyes light up as she talked about her daughter's upcoming volleyball tournament in the West Fjords.

Micro-lessons

  • Renting is always an option: Since I forgot my sneakers at Hvammsvik, I had to find another way to get hiking shoes. As I was browsing Troll.Is, I found I could rent hiking shoes (and other things like rain pants and gloves).

  • Offer to take someone's photo: As a solo traveler, it can be hard to meet people organically. I found it helps to offer a small gesture, like taking someone's photo, to get a conversation going!
